Theory of Computation is a course which is centered around various computational models, such as
DFAs and Turing Machines and their respective limitations.
  • This course began by defining what a "language" is, where a language is a set of all possible outputs of a single computational model.
  • After we were introduced to languages, we began deriving languages using Deterministic Finite Automata(DFA) and creating DFAs to specifically represent a given language. A DFA is essentially a directed graph with accept and reject states, where each transition between two states requires a speciate alphabet symbol.
  • We were then introduced to a concept of Non-deterministic Finita Automata(NFA) and how NFAs are similar to DFAs although they have the added functionality of potentially using an epsilon transitions(Empty string transitions). Alongside NFAs, we were shown how to convert any NFA into the equivalent DFA and we discussed the properties of regular languages.
  • Once we understood NFAs and DFAs, we were then taught about Context-Free-Grammars and Pushdown Automata and how these languages are more powerful than DFAs and NFAs.
  • We then went on to discuss Turing Machine, The different types of Turing Machines, Unrestricted Grammars, as well as the Church-Turing Thesis. While discussing Turing Machines, we also discusses their limitations and the property that all variants of Turing Machines can be represented as a single tape, normal turing machine.
  • Finally, we discussed Computability Theory, ranging from Dedidable Languages, to the Halting Problem, to reducing other Turing Machine problems into the Halting problem to prove undecidability.
